Mechanisms of Evolution

NATURAL SELECTION

Natural selection is the process of gradual evolution where the organisms best suited to the environment tend survive and produce offspring. This is known as the process of natural selection that was advanced by Charles Darwin to explain how change occurs in species over time or across generations.

Charles Darwin was a British naturalist, born in Shrewsbury, England, in 1809 and passing away in Kent in 1882. He came from a family of intellectuals, being the grandson of Erasmus Darwin and Josiah Wedgwood. Darwin studied medicine at the University of Edinburgh and biology at Cambridge. He was chosen as a naturalist for the HMS Beagle expedition from 1831 to 1836, which took him to South America and the South Seas. His observations of animals and rocks during this voyage led to many important discoveries and laid the groundwork for his theories of evolution.

I think we all recall that evolution is the process of alteration of the traits of a population over a given period of time. More precisely, it refers to alterations in the gene frequencies in a population or line. Alleles are two or more, different forms of genes that are passed to any organism through sexual and asexual means. For instance, eye color in humans has several alleles meaning that different people have different eye colors, for example, brown, red, black or blue. The combination of alleles is known as genotype, whereas the totality of actual physical features characteristic of the genotype is known as phenotype. It is in this chapter that several factors that change alleles in a population, and how this change may, in the long run, bring about new species, will be discussed.

It is important to recall that natural selection is the main responsible factor behind evolutionary change in population. Charles Darwin and Alfred Russell Wallace made the following observation that organisms within a population forever fight for the few resources available so only some of them reproduce. Genetically endowed individuals will be more likely to live longer and have offspring, meaning their genes will be carried on to the next generation. Because these attributes are genetic, they tend to reoccur in the next generations of female offspring. Organisms with advantageous phenotypic characteristics are regarded as having higher fitness, that is a higher probability of surviving and reproducing.

Almost always, real-life examples of natural selection can take a long while to happen. A particularly clear example is the case of the Galápagos finch birds; in fact, these birds contributed to the development of this theory. The grants first began their work on these finches in 1976, and this research became instrumental in the determination of natural selection. They tracked alterations in the size and form of beaks of medium ground finches in Daphne Major, an island in the Galápagos. These finches feed on seeds while some are depicted as having bulky, deep beaks while others possess thin beaks. Large billed birds are therefore able to exploit large, hard seeds most effectively while those birds which have small bills are more suited to obtaining small, soft seeds.

In 1977, drought limited the availability of seeds at all developmental stages, especially the smaller and less hardened seeds. As a result, those birds that had larger beaks had a higher chance of survival as they could reach food that small-beaked birds could not. The following year, the Grants discovered that on average the size of the beak of the Galapagos finch had changed, thus proving that natural selection had indeed played out in the bird’s favor in relation to the availability of seed. They understood this by using their prior knowledge of how a smaller population of beak size relates to the inheritance of large beaks about the changes in the beak size in the offspring of the larger population of survivors. Further on, the later studies of the scientists indicated that the beak sizes changed successfully over time and were larger when it was advantageous and smaller on the contrary, if the seeds were scarce.

VARIATION AND ADAPTATION

Variation refers to the differences in physical traits and behaviors among individuals within a species, which can be caused by genetic differences or environmental factors. Adaptation is the process by which organisms develop traits that enhance their ability to survive and reproduce in specific environments, often as a result of natural selection acting on variations.

Natural selection can exist only if there is variation among individuals in a population and the variation is heritable; selection will then bring about change in the traits in the successive generations. Variation can also be non-genetic, for example, a person with better nutrition being taller. However, this does not really have any relevance in the evolutionary process.

In general terms, an adaptation can be defined as an innate characteristic possessed by an organism that ensures its functionality and capability of reproducing in a given ecosystem. Fitness subjected to changes results from Genotypes that make changes or remain constant in order to meet the needs of the population as it is found in its environment. For instance, there were changes in the beak of finches in relation to the food resources available to them.

An adaptation is different from an acclimation which only is a temporary change of the phenotype of an organism because of some changing conditions of the environment. For example, a dog in a hot environment will have more of a smooth skin but more skin pigment in a cold environment. This adaptation is known to be short-term and does not form a favorable gene pool for the next generations.

A reaction will therefore be more favorable depending on the environment in which it exists. A trait beneficial in one set of conditions may be disadvantageous in another, as seen with finches: big beaks available in one area while small beaks were suitable in another area in the same environment hence, stabilizing prices was difficult.

GENETIC DRIFT

Genetic drift is a mechanism of evolution that causes random changes in the frequency of alleles in a population, particularly in small populations. This process can lead to significant genetic variation over time, independent of natural selection.

Genetic drift is a random change in allele frequencies within a population. It reduces genetic variation, and its impact is more significant in small populations. If a population were infinitely large, genetic drift would not occur, but all real populations are finite. Genetic drift happens because the offspring generation is a random sample of the parent population's alleles. Factors like individual deaths, partner choice, and gamete fertilization contribute to this randomness.

In small populations, the loss of even a single individual can significantly impact allele frequencies. For example, in a population of ten animals, losing one animal means losing 10% of the gene pool, whereas in a population of 100 animals, losing one is just a 1% loss. Random events like natural disasters can cause these shifts, impacting allele frequencies differently depending on the traits of the survivors.

For instance, if a population starts with an equal number of two alleles, the next generation's allele distribution will vary randomly. Over time, one allele might become fixed (present in all individuals), and the other lost. This stochastic process is more evident in small populations and when allele frequencies are not balanced.

Genetic drift can be intensified by events like population bottlenecks, where a large portion of the population is randomly killed, leaving a genetically different group of survivors. Another example is the founder effect, where a small group migrates and establishes a new population with different allele frequencies than the original population. This effect is seen in the Afrikaner population of South Africa, where certain genetic disorders are more prevalent due to the higher frequency of mutations in the founding colonists.

GENE FLOW

Gene flow is the movement of genes between populations, typically through the migration of individuals. It can introduce new genetic variation into a population and promote genetic diversity within species.

Another mechanism is gene flow –the movement of alleles into and out of a population through the migration of individuals. It is worth noticing that some populations are relatively more or less fixed compared to the other populations. For instance, many plants generate their seeds and release them to distant places through air or in the intestines of animals; the seeds may bring alleles prevalent in the original population to a new population where they are hardly encountered. Like mutation, gene flow also increases the amount of variation that is present in a population because new alleles are introduced when some individuals move into this population.
